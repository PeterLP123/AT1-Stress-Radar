# Methodology

## Called versus extended states

AT1 instruments are perpetual but callable by the issuer, typically first at
around five years after issue and periodically thereafter. Whether the issuer
calls is an economic decision (refinancing cost versus the reset coupon) and
a supervisory one. Rather than model that decision, the prototype values two
transparent deterministic states:

- **`called_at_first_call`** — the instrument is redeemed at par on the
  first call date. Only the remaining fixed coupons and the principal at the
  first call date are paid.
- **`extended_to_next_call`** — the issuer skips the first call once. The
  terminal date is the first call date plus the instrument's
  `subsequent_call_frequency_months`, and redemption at par occurs there.
  Coupons between the first call date and the terminal date pay the reset
  rate.

The difference between the two state values isolates the *extension impact*
under fixed discounting assumptions. Neither state is a prediction; a real
valuation would weight many extension horizons by call probabilities.

## Coupon reset assumption

Fixed-to-reset AT1s pay a fixed coupon until the first call date, then reset
to a benchmark rate (e.g. 5-year mid-swap) plus a contractual margin fixed at
issue. The prototype assumes:

- The reset happens exactly on the first call date; coupon periods starting
  on or after that date pay the reset rate.
- The benchmark rate is a **single user-supplied constant**
  (`assumed_reset_benchmark_rate`), so every post-reset coupon pays
  `assumed benchmark + reset_margin_bps / 10_000`.

In reality the benchmark would be fixed at the reset observation date and
future resets would follow the forward curve. A constant assumption keeps the
first slice transparent and testable.

## Flat discount-rate simplification

All cash flows are discounted with a single flat annual rate `r`:

```
DF(t) = (1 + r) ^ (-t),   t = actual days from valuation to payment / 365.25
```

This collapses the risk-free curve, the credit spread, and any term structure
into one number. Consequences:

- No curve shape: long and short cash flows are discounted at the same rate.
- No separation of rates and credit: a "spread widening" and a "rate rise"
  are indistinguishable to the model (the scenario schema already separates
  them for future work).
- The days/365.25 time measure is a deliberate simplification, independent of
  each instrument's coupon day-count convention.

## Day-count and schedule simplifications

- Coupon dates are generated by adding whole calendar months to the issue
  date, with month-end days clamped. There is no business-day adjustment or
  holiday calendar.
- `ACT_365` is actual days / 365; `THIRTY_360` follows the US 30/360 rule;
  `ACT_ACT` is approximated as actual days / 365.25 rather than the full
  ISDA rule.
- The terminal call date must lie exactly on the coupon schedule; the sample
  instruments are constructed so that it does, and instrument validation
  rejects call terms that are incompatible with that schedule.

## Why the result is not a definitive fair value

The output is called a **simplified state value** (or model value) because it
omits, at minimum:

1. Call-probability weighting across many possible extension horizons.
2. Coupon cancellation risk — AT1 coupons are fully discretionary and can be
   switched off (e.g. under MDA restrictions) without default.
3. Trigger and loss-absorption risk — write-down or conversion at the CET1
   trigger is recorded as a term but never enters the cash flows.
4. A term structure of rates and a market-implied credit spread.
5. Market conventions (settlement, accrued-interest quoting, business days).

Each omission can move the number materially. The outputs are research
illustrations of *relative* sensitivity (e.g. called versus extended, low
versus high discount rate), not statements of worth.
